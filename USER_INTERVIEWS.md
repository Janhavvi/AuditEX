# Interview 1

**Name / initials:** R.K.

**Role and company stage:** Engineering lead AI startup

**Context:** Quick call after discussing AI tooling costs in a founder community.

## Direct quotes

- “We started with ChatGPT subscriptions and somehow ended up with five different AI tools nobody fully owns.”
- “I can see the card charges, but I still can’t tell which tools are actually important.”
- “If this could show duplicate spend in five minutes, I’d use it immediately.”

## Most surprising thing they said

They were less interested in total spend and more interested in identifying overlap between tools. Their main frustration was paying for multiple products solving the same problem.

## What it changed about the design

Originally the dashboard focused heavily on charts and totals. After the interview, the design shifted toward highlighting duplicate subscriptions and unused tooling first.

# Interview 2

**Name / initials:** S.M.

**Role and company stage:** Software Engineer at a multinational technology company

**Context:** Short call about how their team manages AI subscriptions, internal tooling, and software costs across departments.

## Direct quotes

- “The problem isn’t really the amount we spend. It’s that nobody knows which tools are still being used.”
- “Teams keep testing new AI products, but old subscriptions never really disappear.”
- “Most dashboards show too many numbers before telling me what actually needs attention.”
- “I’d trust recommendations more if the tool explained why something looked unnecessary.”

## Most surprising thing they said

Even in a large company with approval systems, AI tools were still getting added informally by different teams. Some subscriptions started as temporary experiments but stayed active simply because nobody reviewed them later.

## What it changed about the design

Before this conversation, the dashboard focused heavily on analytics and detailed breakdowns. After the interview, the product direction became much simpler.

The interface now prioritizes:
- duplicate subscriptions
- inactive tools
- overlapping products
- estimated wasted spend
- quick recommendations users can act on immediately

The onboarding flow was also reduced to fewer steps so users can reach the audit summary faster without filling unnecessary setup fields first.

# Interview 3

**Name / initials:** A.P.

**Role and company stage:** Operations manager at a startup

**Context:** Informal conversation through a college startup network.

## Direct quotes

- “Half our AI subscriptions started as experiments and never got cleaned up.”
- “The problem is nobody wants to manually review receipts every month.”
- “If this integrated with card exports automatically, that would save real time.”

## Most surprising thing they said

They already suspected waste existed but avoided reviewing it because the process itself felt annoying and time-consuming.

## What it changed about the design

This led to prioritizing automated imports and reducing manual setup requirements. The onboarding flow was redesigned to minimize user effort before generating results.

---

# Key Patterns Across Interviews

Several consistent themes appeared across all three conversations:

- Teams adopt AI tools faster than they organize them
- Duplicate subscriptions are common
- Founders care more about clarity than advanced reporting
- Users want actionable recommendations, not raw financial data
- Reducing setup friction is critical for adoption

These interviews pushed the product away from being a generic analytics dashboard and toward becoming a lightweight operational audit tool focused on fast, understandable savings insights.
